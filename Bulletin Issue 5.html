Breaking
Anthropic accidentally exposes nearly 3,000 internal documents in public data store, including draft announcement of its most powerful AI model with significant cybersecurity risks
Fortune · March 26, 2026
What happened
Anthropic, the AI company behind the Claude models that power tools used across K-12, left close to 3,000 unpublished internal documents in a publicly searchable data store due to a human configuration error in its content management system. The exposure was discovered by a Fortune journalist and independently confirmed by two cybersecurity researchers: a senior AI security researcher at LayerX Security and a researcher at the University of Cambridge. Among the exposed documents was a draft product announcement for a new model called Claude Mythos, also referred to internally as Capybara, which Anthropic describes as "by far the most powerful AI model we've ever developed." The draft stated the model is "far ahead of any other AI model in cyber capabilities" and warned it "presages an upcoming wave of models that can exploit vulnerabilities in ways that far outpace the efforts of defenders." Anthropic removed public access after being notified by Fortune on March 26 and confirmed the exposure was due to human error.
Who's affected
This incident has two distinct K-12 implications. First, any district using tools powered by Anthropic's Claude models should understand that their primary AI vendor had a significant data handling failure, even if no district data was exposed in this specific incident. Second, the capabilities described in the leaked model documentation signal a significant escalation in AI-enabled cyberattack risk that will affect districts regardless of which AI vendor they use. The impending availability of models with far greater offensive cybersecurity capabilities will require districts to revisit their current security posture.
Compliance Exposure
The configuration error at Anthropic was not a breach in the traditional sense, and most vendor breach notification clauses would not require disclosure of this type of incident. This exposes a gap in standard vendor contracts: misconfiguration events, data store exposure, and internal document leaks are rarely covered by the breach notification language districts rely on. Federal monitors reviewing vendor oversight would ask: Do your AI vendor contracts require disclosure of security configuration failures, not just data breaches? Did you receive any notification from your AI vendors this week? What is your protocol for learning about vendor security incidents through public reporting rather than direct notification?
Recommended Action
Two actions this week, one for each angle. First, on vendor data handling: pull your contracts with any AI tool vendor and check whether breach notification language covers configuration errors, exposed data stores, and internal document leaks, not just confirmed unauthorized access. If it does not, flag this gap for your next vendor review cycle. Second, on emerging cybersecurity risk: note the capabilities described in the Anthropic leak for your district's annual cybersecurity risk assessment. AI-enabled cyberattacks at a scale and speed that outpaces current defenses are no longer a distant concern. If your district does not have a documented plan for responding to AI-assisted attacks against student data systems, begin that conversation with your IT team and legal counsel now.
Workflow Impact
Vendor Management: Review AI vendor contracts for configuration error disclosure requirements, and Security & Infrastructure: Add AI-enabled attack escalation to annual cybersecurity risk assessment
2
Infinite Campus security incident draws scrutiny over what student data was exposed and whether districts were adequately notified
DataBreaches.Net · March 28, 2026
What happened
On March 18, 2026, an unauthorized actor gained access to an Infinite Campus employee's Salesforce account. Salesforce is the company's internal case management and ticketing system, not the student information system itself. The attacker was ShinyHunters, an extortion group known for targeting Salesforce accounts across hundreds of companies. ShinyHunters posted a final warning on its dark web site on March 24 demanding ransom; Infinite Campus refused to engage and sent breach notifications to customers. The company stated no customer databases or student records were accessed. However, DataBreaches.Net downloaded and reviewed the data tranche and found that support tickets submitted by school staff contained specific student names and disability-related discipline records.
Who's affected
All districts using Infinite Campus. The company serves more than 3,200 school districts and manages data for roughly 11 million students across 46 states. Even districts whose student data was not directly accessed should confirm what information their staff submitted through Infinite Campus support tickets, as that is where student-identifiable information was found.
Compliance Exposure
Even if Infinite Campus's characterization of the incident is accurate, the disclosure that it involved directory information raises FERPA questions. Directory information is still student data and its unauthorized disclosure requires district attention. Federal monitors would ask: When were you notified by Infinite Campus? What categories of student data were potentially exposed? Have you assessed whether a FERPA breach notification obligation was triggered? Do you have a record of what data your district has stored in Infinite Campus and what access controls are in place?
Recommended Action
Contact your Infinite Campus account representative this week and request a written summary of the incident, the categories of data involved, and the vendor's determination of whether a notification obligation was triggered. Do not rely on Infinite Campus's public statement as your documentation. Pull your Infinite Campus contract and confirm what your notification rights are. Log the contact date, the response received, and your district's determination of whether family notification is required. If you are uncertain, consult legal counsel before the end of this week.
Workflow Impact
Student Data Privacy: Request written incident summary from Infinite Campus and document your district's breach notification assessment
3
New York City releases preliminary AI guidelines for schools, opens public comment through May 8, critics say guidance raises more questions than it answers
The 74 · March 27, 2026
What happened
New York City's Department of Education released its preliminary AI guidelines for schools and is accepting public feedback through May 8, 2026. The guidelines cover permissible use of AI by teachers and students, including brainstorming, scheduling, and drafting. Critics, including education researchers and practitioners, say the guidance is too vague on student data protections, does not adequately address how teachers should document AI-assisted work, and fails to define guardrails for high-stakes uses such as grading and assessment. NYC is the largest school district in the United States, making its approach a reference point for districts nationwide.
Who's affected
NYC districts directly and under immediate obligation to respond. All districts should treat the NYC guidelines as a benchmark document and monitor whether their own AI policies are less specific than what the largest district in the country is publishing, even in preliminary form.
Compliance Exposure
Districts without written AI use policies face increasing audit risk as major systems publish guidelines. Federal monitors reviewing Title I or IDEA technology compliance would ask: What is your district's written policy on AI use by teachers and students? How are teachers documenting AI-assisted work? What data protections apply when AI tools process student information? If your district does not have answers on file, a published NYC guideline creates a comparison point that auditors can use.
Recommended Action
Download and review the NYC AI guidelines this week. Compare them to your district's current AI acceptable use policy or, if none exists, use NYC's framework as a starting template. Pay particular attention to the data privacy sections, which are the areas critics have flagged as insufficiently specific. If your district has gaps relative to NYC's preliminary guidance, document them and assign ownership for closing each gap before the end of the school year.
Workflow Impact
Compliance & Reporting: Compare district AI acceptable use policy to NYC guidelines and document gaps

Ohio moves to audit school district cybersecurity plans, signaling state-level accountability pressure on data protection practices
Spectrum News 1 · March 29, 2026
What happened
Ohio is moving forward with audits of school district cybersecurity plans, with Cleveland Metropolitan School District identified as a front-line example of data protection efforts. The audit signals that state education agencies are beginning to treat cybersecurity documentation as an accountability issue, not just an IT function. Districts will need to demonstrate not only that they have cybersecurity plans but that those plans are implemented, updated, and applied to student and parent data specifically.
Who's affected
Ohio districts immediately. All districts in states with federal cybersecurity funding requirements or state data security laws should treat this as a preview of coming audit activity. Districts receiving E-Rate funding, Title IV grants, or state technology funding may already have documentation requirements they are not tracking.
Compliance Exposure
A cybersecurity audit will ask for: a current written cybersecurity plan, evidence the plan has been reviewed and updated within the past 12 months, staff training records on data security, vendor agreements that include data security requirements, and incident response documentation from any security events in the prior year. Districts that cannot produce these documents face findings that can affect funding and create legal exposure.
Recommended Action
Pull your district's current cybersecurity plan and check the last revision date. If it has not been updated in the past 12 months, schedule a review before the end of this school year. Confirm that the plan specifically addresses student data, not just network infrastructure. Check your vendor agreements for data security language and document any gaps. If you have had any security incidents in the past 12 months, confirm that your incident response was documented and retained.
Workflow Impact
Security & Infrastructure: Review and update district cybersecurity plan documentation and confirm student data protections are specifically addressed
5
California introduces sweeping AI chatbot legislation to protect minors, following Pennsylvania's lead and signaling national momentum
State Affairs · March 27, 2026
What happened
California lawmakers introduced legislation to regulate AI companion chatbots used by minors, adding California to a growing list of states moving to legislate AI chatbot interactions with children. The bill follows Pennsylvania's SAFECHAT Act, which passed the Senate 49-1 in March and awaits a House vote. With Pennsylvania and California both advancing legislation, the pattern suggests a wave of state-level AI chatbot regulation is underway that will require districts to document and govern student use of AI chat platforms.
Who's affected
California districts are most immediately affected. All districts nationwide using any AI tool with chat functionality accessible to students should begin documenting what they are using, what age verification exists, and what parental consent has been obtained. These are the questions both the Pennsylvania and California bills will require answers to.
Compliance Exposure
As state legislatures act, the absence of district-level AI chatbot policy becomes an increasingly documentable gap. Federal monitors reviewing Title I or IDEA technology use would ask: What AI chat tools are students using? What parental consent was obtained? What content restrictions does the vendor provide? Are any of these tools designated as AI companions or relationship simulators? Districts that cannot answer these questions are behind the compliance curve on an issue that is accelerating rapidly.
Recommended Action
Inventory every AI tool currently approved for student use that includes a chat or conversational interface. For each one, document: whether it is designed to simulate an ongoing relationship with a user, what age verification the vendor provides, what parental consent your district obtained before deployment, and what content restrictions are in place. This inventory is the foundation of compliance with both the Pennsylvania and California frameworks. Begin it now rather than waiting for final passage.
Workflow Impact
Compliance & Reporting: Inventory all student-facing AI chat tools and document age verification, consent, and content restriction status for each

Prince William County schools classify AI-enabled glasses as cellphones under existing policy, creating a replicable model for districts facing wearable AI questions
WTOP · March 27, 2026
What happened
Prince William County School System in Virginia determined that AI-enabled glasses meet the definition of a cellphone under its existing policy 729, which governs cellphone-free education. The classification means AI glasses are subject to the same restrictions as phones in schools where phone bans are in effect. This is one of the first documented district-level policy decisions specifically addressing wearable AI technology, and it provides a practical model other districts can reference.
Who's affected
All districts with cellphone policies that have not yet addressed wearable AI devices. As consumer AI glasses from Meta, Google, and other manufacturers become more common among students, this gap will become increasingly visible during audits and parent complaints.
Compliance Exposure
A district without a clear policy on wearable AI devices faces ambiguity about whether existing cellphone or electronic device policies apply. That ambiguity creates inconsistent enforcement, which in turn creates equity and discipline documentation concerns. If a student is disciplined for wearing AI glasses in school and the policy does not specifically address them, the district's decision may be challenged.
Recommended Action
Review your current electronic device or cellphone policy language and determine whether it encompasses wearable AI devices by current definition. If not, add explicit language this semester. Prince William County's approach of classifying AI glasses under an existing cellphone definition is the most administratively efficient path and does not require a new standalone policy. Document the policy review and the determination in your policy update log.
Workflow Impact
Compliance & Reporting: Review electronic device policy language to confirm wearable AI devices are covered and document the determination
