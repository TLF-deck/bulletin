This Week's Summary
This week highlights three converging threats: inappropriate AI-generated content reaching elementary students through vetted tools, AI-enhanced phishing attacks bypassing existing security training, and the ongoing fallout from the PowerSchool breach now confirmed as the largest student data breach in U.S. history. Each incident creates immediate documentation and policy review requirements.
Incidents
1
AI phishing attacks bypass traditional email security training in K-12 districts
EdTech Magazine · February 2026
What happened
Generative AI has fundamentally changed phishing threats by dramatically increasing both speed and scale of attacks targeting school systems. Traditional security awareness training is proving insufficient against AI-enhanced social engineering tactics that mimic legitimate district communications.
Who's affected
All districts relying on email security protocols developed before 2024. Particularly affects districts without AI-aware phishing detection tools or updated staff training on AI-generated threats.
Compliance Exposure
FERPA auditors will look for documented security incident logs showing detection methods. Districts must show they've updated Acceptable Use Policies and data breach response plans to address AI-generated attacks. Cybersecurity insurance may deny claims if policies haven't been updated to address known AI phishing risks.
Recommended Action
This week: Pull last 90 days of email security logs. Document any suspected AI-generated phishing attempts. Schedule emergency IT meeting to review current filtering tools' AI detection capabilities. Flag for legal review: Does your cyber insurance policy explicitly cover AI-generated attacks?
Workflow Impact
Security & Infrastructure: Review email filtering contracts and staff security training modules for AI threat coverage
2
Adobe Express for Education generated sexualized images for elementary students in California
CalMatters · February 26, 2026
What happened
Adobe's AI tool, accessed through the Schoology learning management system for a 4th grade book project, generated inappropriate sexualized images in response to student prompts. The incident occurred as California released new state guidelines for AI safety in schools.
Who's affected
All districts using Adobe Express for Education or similar AI content generation tools in K-5 settings. Specifically impacts LAUSD and any district that approved Adobe tools through Schoology integration without separate AI content filter review.
Compliance Exposure
CIPA requires districts to block harmful content to minors. AI-generated content may bypass traditional filtering. Auditors will ask: When was the tool vetted? Who approved it? What content filter testing occurred? Does the vendor agreement include liability for AI-generated inappropriate content? Title IX coordinators may investigate if students were exposed to sexual content in school-provided technology.
Recommended Action
Immediate: Pull list of all AI content generation tools accessible through your LMS. Verify each has child safety filters distinct from general content moderation. Test with grade-level appropriate prompts. Document results. Send vendor inquiry to Adobe and any similar tools: 'What child safety filters are active? How are they tested? What is our liability under our agreement if inappropriate content is generated?' Pause elementary access to AI generation tools until written vendor responses received.
Workflow Impact
Instructional Technology: Emergency review of all LMS-integrated AI tools for child safety filter documentation and testing protocols
3
PowerSchool breach confirmed as largest student data breach in U.S. history: 62M student records exposed
TechPolicy.Press · February 2026
What happened
PowerSchool detected unauthorized access in December 2024 affecting 16,000 schools. By January 2025, confirmed exfiltration included over 62 million student records and nearly 10 million teacher records. Full scope and notification timeline continues through February 2026.
Who's affected
Any district using PowerSchool Student Information System between December 2024 and breach remediation. Particularly affects districts that haven't completed required breach notifications to families or documented their response for federal compliance reviews.
Compliance Exposure
FERPA requires districts to document breach response even when vendor is at fault. State breach notification laws (vary by state) require parent notification within specific timeframes. OCR may audit: When did you learn of breach? When did you notify families? What documentation exists of your vendor oversight? What alternative access controls were implemented? Did breach affect IEP/504 records (IDEA) or ELL records (Title III)?
Recommended Action
If you use PowerSchool: Pull all breach notification documentation sent to families. Create folder with: (1) Timeline of when district learned of breach, (2) Copies of all parent notifications sent, (3) Vendor communications, (4) Board meeting minutes where breach was discussed, (5) Any credit monitoring offered. If you haven't notified families yet and breach affects your instance: Legal counsel required this week. If you don't use PowerSchool: Document in your SIS vendor contract file 'PowerSchool breach February 2026' as justification for enhanced vendor security review in next contract cycle.
Workflow Impact
Student Data Privacy: Compile complete breach response documentation file for potential OCR audit of vendor oversight and parent notification compliance
Policy Watch

California released new state guidelines for AI safety in schools in February 2026, directly responding to the Adobe incident. Guidelines address AI content generation tools used by minors and likely preview requirements that will appear in other states' legislative sessions through spring 2026.

Begin reviewing: Pull current AI tool approval process. Compare against California's new guidelines even if you're out-of-state—these often become national compliance baseline.

Anti-screentime movement expanding scope to treat educational technology with same regulatory scrutiny as consumer social media. EdTech products using AI with minors may face California consumer protection regulations in addition to education-specific rules.

No immediate action required. Monitor for state bills introduced in your legislature that reference 'screentime limits' or 'social media age verification'—these may inadvertently include educational tools in scope.
