Incidents
1
Adobe Express for Education generated sexually explicit images for 4th grade students in LAUSD classroom assignment
CalMatters · February 26, 2026
What happened
Adobe's AI tool, distributed through LAUSD's Schoology learning management system and marketed as education-safe, generated sexualized images when elementary students entered prompts for a book project. The incident occurred despite the tool being positioned as appropriate for unsupervised classroom use and triggered immediate state-level response on AI vetting standards.
Who's affected
All districts using Adobe Express for Education or similar generative AI tools approved for unsupervised student use. Particularly relevant for districts serving grades K-5 and those with AI tools embedded in learning management systems.
Compliance Exposure
CIPA requires districts to block harmful visual content. If a vetted tool bypasses content filters, auditors will ask: What was the vetting process? Who approved unsupervised use? What monitoring occurred? Districts must document that they performed reasonable due diligence on AI tool safety claims before classroom deployment. Absence of vendor AI safety certifications or internal testing protocols creates documentation gaps for CIPA compliance and duty-of-care obligations.
Recommended Action
By March 9: Pull usage logs for all generative AI tools with image generation capability approved for grades K-5. Verify whether any are cleared for unsupervised use. If yes, require adult preview of all AI-generated outputs until vendor provides updated safety certification. Document this review and interim control measure. Add 'AI content generation safety testing' as required line item in edtech vetting rubric.
Workflow Impact
Instructional Technology: Add AI content safety review gate to all tools with generative capabilities before K-5 deployment
2
AI-enhanced phishing campaigns target K-12 sector with unprecedented speed and personalization
EdTech Magazine · February 2026
What happened
Generative AI has fundamentally changed the phishing threat landscape for schools by enabling attackers to produce convincing, personalized messages at scale and speed. Security researchers identify education as especially vulnerable due to limited IT security budgets and large attack surfaces created by BYOD policies and third-party tool integrations.
Who's affected
All districts. Heightened risk for districts with: decentralized purchasing, frequent parent communications via email, BYOD programs, limited IT security staff, or high staff turnover creating gaps in security awareness training.
Compliance Exposure
Under FERPA and state breach notification laws, districts must demonstrate 'reasonable security measures.' If a phishing attack leads to unauthorized access of student records, auditors will examine: staff training logs, email security configurations, multi-factor authentication deployment, incident response protocols. AI-enhanced phishing creates higher baseline for 'reasonable' since the threat has measurably increased. Districts using 2023-era security awareness training may not meet current threat standard.
Recommended Action
By March 9: Schedule 15-minute executive briefing with IT director and superintendent to confirm: (1) Current email security posture accounts for AI-generated phishing, (2) MFA is enforced for all systems containing student data, (3) Staff completed security awareness training in past 6 months that specifically addresses AI-enhanced social engineering. If any answer is no, draft 60-day remediation timeline. Document this review for audit trail.
Workflow Impact
Security & Infrastructure: Audit whether current security awareness training addresses AI-enhanced phishing tactics
3
California positions itself as national AI regulator for children's products as screentime policy converges with AI oversight
EdTech Insiders · January 20, 2026
What happened
California is establishing itself as the de facto national standard-setter for AI in children's products, bringing both Silicon Valley AI companies and edtech under unified regulatory framework. This coincides with expansion of screentime concerns from social media to include instructional technology, creating dual regulatory pressure on digital learning tools.
Who's affected
All districts, particularly those procuring AI-enabled tools or facing parent advocacy around screentime. Districts in states without AI-specific legislation will likely follow California standards as vendors adopt single compliance approach.
Compliance Exposure
While no immediate compliance requirement exists, California's regulatory approach typically becomes vendor baseline nationally (see CCPA precedent). Districts should anticipate: mandatory AI impact assessments for student-facing tools, enhanced age-verification requirements, algorithmic transparency documentation, and screentime reporting. Districts that wait to document AI tool inventory and usage purposes will face retrospective documentation demands when regulations formalize. Board policies silent on AI governance create policy gap if parent questions arise.
Recommended Action
By March 16: Schedule board policy review session for April meeting. Agenda item: 'AI in Instructional Technology: Current Inventory and Governance Framework.' Ask curriculum and IT teams to jointly prepare: complete list of AI-enabled tools in use, grade levels served, and primary learning purpose. Use this to identify policy gaps before regulatory requirements formalize. This is documentation-building, not immediate compliance action.
Workflow Impact
Vendor Management: Create AI tool inventory with documented learning purpose as foundation for coming regulatory requirements
