This Week's Summary
This week produced three incidents that define where K-12 student data liability is heading. The California Supreme Court ruled in J.M. v. Illuminate Education that a student whose medical records were exposed in a 2021 breach could not proceed under the state's medical privacy and customer records laws because Illuminate is not a health care provider and students are not its customers. The ruling is narrow, but the court's clarification that unauthorized exposure creates a breach even without confirmed unauthorized viewing is a significant signal for how K-12 districts should think about what they must disclose. Separately, Siloam Springs School District in Arkansas accidentally posted an interactive map of student addresses and contact information to its public website for 17 hours while trying to share transportation zone information with parents, with Superintendent Shane Patrick issuing a public apology and Communications Director Aaron Russell calling it plain and simple human error. And the Michigan Department of Education released AI guidance on May 12 that is the most operationally useful state framework published so far, with a starter guide, a comprehensive administrative framework, and specific role-based templates for district leaders, principals, and teachers.
Incidents
1
Breaking
California Supreme Court rules in J.M. v. Illuminate Education that edtech vendors are not health care providers and students are not their customers under state privacy law, but clarifies that unauthorized exposure alone constitutes a breach even without confirmed viewing
California Supreme Court, Law360, Bloomberg Law · May 14-15, 2026
What happened
On May 14, 2026, the California Supreme Court issued its ruling in J.M. v. Illuminate Education, Inc. (S286699), reversing the Court of Appeal's decision and dismissing the case on the specific claims brought. The plaintiff, J.M., was an 11-year-old student whose personal and medical records were exposed in Illuminate's 2021 data breach. The breach was discovered in December 2021 after a hacker used the login credentials of a former Illuminate employee that had never been deactivated. Illuminate did not notify affected students and districts for five months. J.M. brought class action claims under California's Confidentiality of Medical Information Act and the Customer Records Act. The Supreme Court held that Illuminate does not qualify as a "provider of health care" under the CMIA because it is an educational technology company, not a health care entity, and that J.M. is not Illuminate's "customer" under the CRA because students do not do business with Illuminate directly. Their school districts do. On these grounds the claims were dismissed. However, the court made a significant clarifying statement on the CMIA: a breach of confidentiality under that statute occurs when medical information is exposed to a significant risk of unauthorized access or use. Actual confirmed viewing by an unauthorized party is not required. The court remanded to the Court of Appeal to determine whether J.M. should be permitted to file an amended complaint. DataBreaches.Net noted that the case may not be over.
Who's affected
California districts directly, and all districts nationally that use edtech vendors handling student medical information, including health records, IEP data, disability status, and social-emotional data. The ruling resolves the specific question of whether Illuminate is a CMIA health care provider. It does not resolve whether students can pursue other legal theories, whether Illuminate's contractual obligations to districts were met, or whether similar conduct would be treated differently under FERPA or other federal frameworks. Districts that used Illuminate during the 2021 breach period should note that the case has not ended, only this appeal chapter of it.
Compliance Exposure
The court's clarification on the CMIA breach standard applies broadly: unauthorized exposure, not confirmed viewing, is sufficient to trigger a breach. For districts, this means that any configuration error, accidental public posting, or vendor misconfiguration that exposes student medical information to a risk of unauthorized access may constitute a breach under California law even if no confirmed unauthorized access occurred. This is directly relevant to the Missouri MOScholars incident from last month, the Siloam Springs incident this week, and the Anthropic configuration error in March. Federal monitors reviewing FERPA vendor oversight would ask: What is your district's policy for determining whether a breach notification obligation has been triggered when student data is exposed but not confirmed to have been accessed?
Recommended Action
Two actions this week. First, districts in California that used Illuminate Education during the 2021-2022 school year: review whether your students were part of the class and monitor the remanded case for any amended complaint that may proceed. Contact your legal counsel to confirm your district's exposure is documented. Second, all districts: update your breach assessment procedures to reflect the court's clarification that exposure to unauthorized access risk, not confirmed unauthorized viewing, is sufficient to trigger a notification analysis. If your district's breach protocol requires confirmed access before initiating a notification assessment, that standard is inconsistent with how California courts are interpreting privacy law, and likely inconsistent with how FERPA regulators will approach similar situations.
Workflow Impact
Student Data Privacy: Update breach assessment procedures to reflect that unauthorized exposure risk, not confirmed access, triggers a notification analysis under California law and consistent federal principles
2
Siloam Springs School District posts interactive map of student names, addresses, and contact information on public website for 17 hours while trying to share transportation zone information with parents
5NEWS, Arkansas Democrat-Gazette · May 14-16, 2026
What happened
Siloam Springs Public Schools in Arkansas accidentally posted an interactive map to its public website containing student and parent names, home addresses, and other contact information as part of a transportation zone planning program for the 2026-27 school year. The map displayed pinpoint markers; when a user clicked a pinpoint, the associated student and family contact information was visible. District Communications Director Aaron Russell confirmed the data was online for approximately 17 hours before being taken down. Superintendent Shane Patrick issued a public apology on the district's Facebook page, writing that an internal document containing district demographic information was inadvertently posted to the district webpage. Russell stated the incident was the result of human error, not a malicious breach, saying: "It was just simple human error. It was just plain and simple, a mistake, and we want to own that mistake." The district said it reviewed its procedures and safeguards after removing the data.
Who's affected
Siloam Springs families whose student and contact data was visible on the public map. All districts using interactive mapping tools, geographic information systems, or data visualization platforms to share program information with parents should treat this as a direct operational risk. The mistake was not in the intent, which was to communicate with families about transportation zones, but in the execution: live student data was embedded in a file posted to a public-facing web property without a review step to confirm what the file contained.
Compliance Exposure
This incident is the third in six weeks involving student data embedded in files posted to public websites: Missouri's MOScholars spreadsheet in April, the CoSN conference's CC-versus-BCC scenario in April, and now Siloam Springs. The pattern is identical in each case: staff intend to share non-sensitive information and unknowingly include student personally identifiable information in the file or map. Under FERPA, the intent of the staff member is not a defense. Posting student home addresses and contact information to a public website without authorization is a FERPA violation regardless of why it happened. Under the California Supreme Court's clarification issued this same week, exposure risk alone, not confirmed access, is sufficient to trigger a breach analysis. Arkansas has its own breach notification statute, and Siloam Springs' 17-hour exposure window creates a notification assessment obligation. Federal monitors would ask: What is your district's procedure for reviewing publicly posted files before they go live? Who is responsible for confirming that no student data is embedded in public-facing documents?
Recommended Action
This week, establish or confirm a review step in your district's web publishing procedure that requires any file, map, or document posted to a public-facing website to be opened and reviewed for embedded student data before it goes live. This review does not need to be time-consuming. It needs to exist and be documented. The specific failure at Siloam Springs, a data-embedded interactive map, should be added to your staff FERPA training materials as a live example. If your district uses any geographic mapping or data visualization tool to communicate with families, confirm that the live student data layer is disabled before any file generated from that system is exported for public use.
Workflow Impact
Student Data Privacy: Establish a documented pre-publication review step for all files posted to public-facing websites, and add data-embedded map exposure to staff FERPA training materials
3
Michigan Department of Education releases AI guidance on May 12 with a starter guide, a comprehensive administrative framework, and role-based templates for district leaders, principals, and teachers
Michigan Department of Education, WILX, WWJ Newsradio 950 · May 12-16, 2026
What happened
On May 12, 2026, the Michigan Department of Education released new AI guidance for K-12 districts. State Superintendent Dr. Glenn Maleyko said: "AI can be a good learning and teaching tool if used properly. But we need to make sure our educators and our students understand AI and know how to use it appropriately." Deputy Superintendent Dr. Delsa Chapman, who led the guidance development, described it as a comprehensive guide that provides Michigan school districts with structured administrative guidelines for implementing responsible AI practices across organizational roles, policy, and operations. The guidance includes a starter guide for districts that have not yet addressed AI and a comprehensive framework for districts further along. It identifies six essential practice areas: keep AI purposeful and safe; protect privacy and integrity; build AI literacy; maintain human oversight; promote equity and accessibility; and support transparency and continuous improvement. The framework includes practical examples and role-specific templates for district administrators, building principals, and teachers. Williamston Community Schools Superintendent Adam Spina said his district has already spent two years building an AI implementation framework and found the MDE guidance consistent with the work they have done.
Who's affected
Michigan districts immediately. All districts nationally should review Michigan's framework because it is the most operationally complete state AI guidance released to date. It is not limited to a list of prohibited uses or a policy statement. It provides role-specific templates districts can adapt immediately. Districts that have been waiting for their state to publish guidance before acting now have a well-developed reference document from a state that has moved ahead of most others.
Compliance Exposure
The MDE guidance's emphasis on human oversight and the explicit requirement to protect privacy and integrity address the two compliance gaps that have generated the most federal enforcement activity this year: AI used in IEP decisions without appropriate team involvement, and AI tools deployed to students without documented data privacy agreements. Districts that have not yet addressed these two areas in writing are operating behind a standard that is now published state guidance in Michigan and operational guidance in New York City. Federal monitors reviewing Title I technology compliance or IDEA implementation would ask: What is your district's documented standard for human oversight of AI in educational decisions? What data privacy documentation exists for each AI tool used with students?
Recommended Action
Download Michigan's AI guidance this week and use it as a self-assessment tool. Work through the six essential practice areas and identify which ones your district has addressed in writing and which it has not. Pay particular attention to the human oversight and privacy protection sections, since those map directly to active federal enforcement priorities. If your district is in Michigan, the guidance is now your reference standard and your legal counsel should review your current policies against it. If your district is outside Michigan, the guidance is the most complete public template available for building your own framework before the end of the school year.
Workflow Impact
Instructional Technology: Use Michigan's AI guidance as a self-assessment framework and address any gaps in human oversight documentation and student data privacy agreements before end of school year
