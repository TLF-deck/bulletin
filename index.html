This Week's Summary
This week marks three converging threats for K-12 technology leaders: AI tools generating inappropriate content in classroom settings, sophisticated phishing campaigns targeting districts, and the continued fallout from the PowerSchool breach now affecting compliance documentation. California's regulatory response signals a shift from reactive policies to proactive vetting requirements that will affect procurement nationwide.
Incidents
1
Adobe Express for Education Generated Sexualized Images in 4th Grade Classroom Assignment
CalMatters / Daily Caller · February 26, 2026
What happened
Adobe's AI tool, accessed through the Schoology learning management system, produced inappropriate sexualized images when elementary students entered prompts for a book project. The incident occurred despite the tool being marketed as education-safe and prompted California to release new AI safeguard guidelines.
Who's affected
All districts using Adobe Express for Education through Schoology or similar LMS platforms. Particularly impacts elementary schools (K-5) where unsupervised AI use is common.
Compliance Exposure
CIPA requires districts to monitor technology that may expose minors to harmful content. Federal monitors will look for: (1) documentation of content filtering policies covering AI tools, (2) supervision protocols for AI use by age group, (3) incident response records showing when inappropriate content was flagged and how quickly access was restricted. Districts may face questions about whether they conducted their own testing before deployment.
Recommended Action
This week: Audit all AI-enabled tools accessible through your LMS. For each tool with generative AI features, document: age-appropriate use restrictions, supervision requirements, and content filtering mechanisms. Suspend unsupervised elementary student access to Adobe Express AI features until supervision protocols are established. Add 'AI content generation' as a specific category in your acceptable use policy with age-based restrictions.
Workflow Impact
Instructional Technology: Review LMS-integrated tool vetting process to include AI content generation testing before classroom deployment
2
AI-Generated Phishing Attacks Against K-12 Districts Accelerating in Speed and Scale
EdTech Magazine · February 2026
What happened
Security researchers report AI is enabling attackers to automate sophisticated phishing campaigns in hours instead of weeks, dramatically increasing both volume and credibility of attacks targeting school districts. AI allows rapid creation of convincing impersonation emails that bypass traditional detection methods.
Who's affected
All districts. Particularly vulnerable are business offices handling vendor payments, HR departments processing employee data, and any staff with access to student information systems.
Compliance Exposure
Under FERPA, districts must use 'reasonable methods' to protect student records from unauthorized disclosure. A successful phishing attack leading to data exposure creates questions about whether security training was current, whether multi-factor authentication was required for sensitive systems, and whether incident response documentation exists. State breach notification laws require specific timelines—delays in detection extend compliance exposure.
Recommended Action
This week: Schedule mandatory phishing simulation training for all staff with email access by end of March. Require multi-factor authentication on all systems containing student or employee PII by April 1. Update incident response plan to include 'AI-generated phishing' as a threat category with specific detection indicators. Distribute one-page guide to business office and HR staff on verifying requests for sensitive information or payments, emphasizing phone verification of requests received by email.
Workflow Impact
Security & Infrastructure: Implement monthly phishing simulations and track click-through rates by department to identify training gaps
3
PowerSchool Breach Impact Now Documented at 62 Million Student Records and 10 Million Teacher Records
TechPolicy.Press · February 2026
What happened
New reporting confirms the December 2024 PowerSchool breach, first detected in January 2025, exposed 62 million student records and nearly 10 million teacher records—now recognized as the largest breach of children's data in U.S. history. The scope continues to expand as districts complete forensic reviews.
Who's affected
Districts using PowerSchool Student Information System. Also affects any district that shared data with PowerSchool clients through state reporting systems or data consortiums.
Compliance Exposure
Districts face ongoing documentation requirements even if they've completed initial breach notification. Federal auditors reviewing Title I or IDEA compliance may request: (1) evidence of updated data sharing agreements with PowerSchool reflecting post-breach security measures, (2) documentation that affected families received second notification about expanded breach scope, (3) records showing review of what specific data elements were exposed and whether credit monitoring was offered where appropriate, (4) current vendor risk assessment for PowerSchool showing why continued use is justified.
Recommended Action
This week: If your district uses PowerSchool, request written confirmation from PowerSchool of exactly which data elements from your district were included in the breach. Document this in your vendor compliance file. If the expanded scope (62M students vs. earlier estimates) includes additional records from your district, send updated notification to affected families by March 14. Schedule vendor risk review meeting before end of March to document decision to continue or discontinue PowerSchool, including alternative vendors evaluated.
Workflow Impact
Vendor Management: Create breach impact documentation file for PowerSchool showing ongoing monitoring and family notification compliance
