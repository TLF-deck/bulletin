Issue 001  ·  Week of March 3, 2026
This week, three incidents that all point to the same gap: districts are adopting AI faster than they are documenting their governance of it. The LAUSD federal probe is the headline, but the Adobe elementary school incident is the one I'd flag for your tech coordinator today — if your district uses any student-facing AI tool and doesn't have a written output review policy, that's your action item this week.

This Week's Summary
This week's incidents center on a common theme: the gap between AI tool adoption speed and institutional oversight capacity. An LAUSD federal probe highlights procurement accountability for AI contracts, an Adobe AI image generation incident exposes the risk of deploying student-facing AI without output review policies, and a Pennsylvania district's handling of AI-generated deepfakes raises questions about incident response documentation.
Incidents
1
FBI Searches LAUSD Superintendent's Home Over AI Contract
KTLA / CalMatters  ·  February 26, 2026
What happened
FBI agents executed search warrants at the home and office of LAUSD Superintendent Alberto Carvalho. Reporting indicates the investigation is connected to a multimillion-dollar contract between LAUSD and AllHere, a now-defunct AI startup that developed a chatbot called "Ed." The probe appears focused on the superintendent personally, not the district.
Who's affected
LAUSD directly. All districts that have contracted with AI startups or sole-source vendors for AI-powered student-facing tools. Particular concern for districts that fast-tracked AI contracts during 2023–2024 without standard procurement review.
Compliance Exposure
This incident surfaces procurement documentation risk. If your district awarded an AI tool contract, a federal monitor or state reviewer could ask: Was the contract competitively bid? Was the vendor's financial viability assessed? Was there a DPA in place before student data was shared? Is there documentation of board approval?
Recommended Action
Pull your district's AI-related contracts from the past two years. For each one, verify: (1) procurement method is documented, (2) a signed DPA exists, (3) board approval is on record, and (4) the vendor is still operational. If any contract lacks these four items, flag it for your superintendent and legal counsel this week.
Workflow Impact
Compliance & Reporting — Review AI tool procurement documentation. Confirm contract files include DPA, board approval, and vendor viability records.
2
Adobe Express AI Tool Generates Inappropriate Images in LA Elementary School
EdSurge  ·  February 28, 2026
What happened
An AI image generation feature in Adobe Express produced inappropriate content when used by elementary students in a Los Angeles school. The incident surfaced after a teacher reported the output to district IT. Adobe has since updated its content moderation settings, but the default configuration at the time allowed the generation to proceed.
Who's affected
All districts using Adobe Express for Education, particularly those with elementary or middle school deployments. Districts that enabled AI features without reviewing content moderation settings are most exposed.
Compliance Exposure
Districts have a duty of care obligation when deploying AI tools with student-facing generative features. If a parent or state reviewer asks whether content moderation was reviewed before deployment, what documentation exists? Was the tool on your approved list before it was assigned to students?
Recommended Action
Log into your Adobe Express for Education admin console this week. Confirm content moderation is set to maximum restriction for student accounts. Document the setting, the date you checked it, and who verified it. Add Adobe Express to your next tool review cycle.
Workflow Impact
Instructional Technology — Review AI feature settings for all student-facing creative tools. Confirm content moderation policies are documented and current.
3
Pennsylvania District Faces Questions Over AI Deepfake Incident Response
The74  ·  March 1, 2026
What happened
A Pennsylvania school district is under scrutiny after AI-generated deepfake images of students circulated among peers. Questions have been raised not about the incident itself — which was handled by administrators — but about whether the district had a documented response protocol in place before it happened, and whether the response was consistent with existing student privacy policies.
Who's affected
All K-12 districts. Deepfake incidents involving students are no longer rare. Any district without a documented AI-generated content incident response protocol has a documentation gap regardless of whether an incident has occurred.
Compliance Exposure
If your district faces a similar incident, a state reviewer or parent attorney could ask: Did your acceptable use policy address AI-generated content before this happened? Was there a documented escalation path? Were student privacy obligations followed in the response? The absence of a pre-existing protocol is itself the finding.
Recommended Action
Check your current acceptable use policy for any mention of AI-generated content or deepfakes. If it's absent, flag it for your next policy review and add an interim guidance memo to student and staff handbooks. Assign ownership of the protocol to a specific role — not just "administration."
Workflow Impact
Student Data Privacy — Review acceptable use policy for AI-generated content coverage. Confirm incident response protocol exists and is assigned to a named role.
