This Week's Summary
This week produced two AI deepfake incidents at K-12 schools within three weeks of each other, a $17.25 million PowerSchool Naviance settlement revealing that roughly 10 million students were in the breach class without district awareness, and a federal budget proposal that eliminates Title II, Title III, Title IV, and after-school funding while preserving Title I-A at current levels. The deepfake incidents, at Radnor High School in Pennsylvania in March and now Lake Zurich High School in Illinois, confirm this is a national pattern requiring immediate policy action. Breaking
Police investigate AI-generated nude images of Lake Zurich High School students, second deepfake incident at a K-12 school in three weeks
NBC 5 Chicago, Chicago Tribune · April 3, 2026
What happened
Lake Zurich High School in Illinois, part of Lake Zurich Community Unit School District 95, is working with police after students used AI image generation tools to create explicit nude images of their classmates. Superintendent Dr. Kelley Gallt notified families April 3, stating the district first received a report on February 26, 2026. School officials describe the content as "very realistic." Police are actively investigating. This follows the Radnor High School incident covered in Issue 004, where Pennsylvania parents met with the governor in March to demand state action. Two confirmed AI deepfake incidents at K-12 schools in three weeks, in different states, involving student-generated content targeting other students.
Who's affected
All districts, particularly those without explicit policies prohibiting the creation or distribution of AI-generated images of students, and those without documented protocols for how administrators, counselors, and law enforcement should respond when an incident occurs.
Compliance Exposure
A district that cannot demonstrate a written policy prohibiting AI-generated sexual content involving students, a documented response protocol, and staff training on that protocol faces significant exposure under Title IX. Federal monitors reviewing Title IX compliance would ask: Is nonconsensual AI-generated imagery explicitly addressed in your harassment and sexual misconduct policy? What is the documented response chain when an incident is reported? Were victim support services provided and documented? Was law enforcement contacted and when? Districts that treated this as an IT or disciplinary issue rather than a Title IX matter face the highest risk.
Recommended Action
Three actions this week. First, confirm your Title IX policy explicitly addresses AI-generated sexual imagery of students. "Harassment" language that predates generative AI may not be specific enough to cover this. Second, document your incident response protocol: who is notified, in what order, what law enforcement contact looks like, and what victim support services are offered. This protocol should exist in writing before the next incident, not after. Third, confirm your acceptable use policy explicitly prohibits the creation or distribution of AI-generated images of students or staff using any device, on or off campus. If it does not, update it this week and distribute it.
Workflow Impact
Compliance & Reporting: Update Title IX policy and acceptable use policy to explicitly address AI-generated sexual imagery, and document the incident response protocol in writing

PowerSchool Naviance reaches $17.25 million settlement in student data breach class action, with roughly 10 million students in the nationwide class and districts unaware their students were affected
K-12 Dive, Fox31 KDVR · April 3, 2026
What happened
PowerSchool's Naviance college counseling platform has reached a $17.25 million settlement in a class action lawsuit over a 2024 data breach. The settlement class covers any student who logged into Naviance at least once between August 18, 2021 and January 23, 2026, a group that reportedly totals roughly 10 million students nationwide. The original plaintiff was a Chicago Public Schools student. This week, Cherry Creek School District in Colorado was alerted when students received settlement notification emails and families asked the district what was happening. Cherry Creek confirmed the emails were legitimate and told families the settlement did not reflect a breach of the district's own systems. Reporting from Fox31 KDVR raised the question of why districts were not notified of the breach when it occurred rather than learning about it through a settlement notification their students received.
Who's affected
Any district using Naviance. If your students logged into Naviance between August 2021 and January 2026, they are likely in the settlement class. K-12 Dive reports that legal observers say the settlement marks how far student data privacy litigation has advanced, with courts now treating edtech data breaches as a serious and compensable harm.
Compliance Exposure
The Cherry Creek situation illustrates a specific gap: districts learned about a breach affecting their students through a settlement notice rather than a vendor breach notification. Federal monitors reviewing FERPA compliance and vendor oversight would ask: Did you receive timely notification from Naviance when the breach occurred? What data does Naviance hold on your students, and what breach notification obligations does your contract require? What is your protocol when students or families alert the district to a vendor security event the district was not informed about directly?
Recommended Action
If your district uses or has used Naviance, contact PowerSchool this week to confirm whether your students are in the settlement class and what steps the company is taking to notify affected families. Pull your Naviance contract and document the breach notification terms. If the contract does not require timely vendor notification of security incidents, flag that gap for your next renewal. For all edtech vendor contracts: confirm breach notification language requires direct district notification, not just public disclosure or settlement, as the trigger.
Workflow Impact
Vendor Management: Confirm Naviance settlement class status for your students and review vendor contracts for direct breach notification requirements
3
Trump budget proposal calls for major cuts to Title I-A and federal education programs, drawing immediate scrutiny from district administrators and federal program directors
Education Week · April 3, 2026
What happened
The Trump administration released its fiscal year 2027 budget proposal on April 4, 2026, calling for major cuts to federal education programs. Contrary to some expectations, the proposal preserves Title I-A at its current level of roughly $18 billion. However, it proposes eliminating Title II professional development funding ($2.2 billion), Title III English Language Acquisition grants ($890 million), Title IV student enrichment grants ($1.4 billion), 21st Century Community Learning Centers ($1.3 billion), the Institute of Education Sciences (cut from over $700 million to $261 million), and the Office for Civil Rights (cut by 35 percent). The proposal is not law and must pass Congress, which rejected comparable cuts last year.
Who's affected
Districts that rely on Title II for professional development, Title III for English Learner services, Title IV for technology and enrichment programs, and 21st Century funds for before and after school programs face the most direct impact if Congress adopts the proposal. IDEA Part B funding would be essentially flat once accounting for the consolidation of subprograms. Districts in states where these eliminated programs provide significant support should begin contingency planning now.
Compliance Exposure
Budget proposals create compliance pressure in two ways. First, if funding is reduced, districts must document any changes to programs funded with federal dollars, including how they will maintain services to students with disabilities and English Learners who have legally enforceable rights to services regardless of funding levels. Second, any staff reductions tied to federal funding changes trigger documentation requirements under applicable employment law and, if SPED staff are involved, IEP team obligations. Federal monitors would ask: What is your contingency plan if Title I funding is reduced? How will you maintain FAPE for students with disabilities if IDEA funding is cut?
Recommended Action
This week, identify every staff position, program, and purchase order in your district that is funded wholly or in part by Title I-A. If you do not have this list current and organized by funding source, building it now is the most important compliance action you can take before the end of the school year. Separately, confirm with your special education director that your district has a documented plan for maintaining FAPE if IDEA funding is reduced, since student entitlements under IDEA do not disappear when federal appropriations change. Do not wait for a final budget to begin this analysis.
Workflow Impact
Compliance & Reporting: Compile a current inventory of all positions and programs funded by Title I-A and confirm a contingency plan exists for maintaining federally required services
